GR20 lower body workout plan — exercises by training phase with sets, reps, and coaching notes

Guided mode off

Build movement patterns, wake up stabilisers, and establish the habit. All bodyweight or lightest dumbbells. Focus entirely on form — especially the hinge pattern and slow eccentric on step-downs.

Warm-up · ~15 min
Hip circle + leg swings mobility
2 × 10 each directionNo rest

Stand holding the bench. Swing each leg forward/back 10 times, then side to side 10 times. Follow with 10 hip circles each direction. Loosens hip flexors and glutes before loading.

Glute bridge glute
2 × 15 repsRest 60 s

Lie on your back, feet flat, knees bent. Drive hips up by squeezing glutes — not by arching your lower back. Hold 1 second at the top. Back safety: the movement is pure hip extension; your lumbar spine stays neutral throughout. If you feel it in your lower back, you're either overextending or your feet are too close.

Main block · ~55 min
Bench step-up with slow step-down eccentric
3 × 10 each legRest 90 sBodyweight

Why it matters: the slow step-down (3–4 seconds) is the most important part — it trains the eccentric quad control that prevents knee pain on descents.

Form: Plant one foot fully on the bench. Drive up through the heel. At the top, stand tall. Then lower the free leg s-l-o-w-l-y back to the floor. Do not let your torso pitch forward. Keep chest tall. Alternate legs each rep or complete all reps on one side.

Back note: holding dumbbells at your sides (not overhead) keeps spinal load minimal. Stay upright — forward lean loads the lumbar.

Reverse lunge eccentric
3 × 10 each legRest 90 sBodyweight

Step backward (not forward) — this loads the front knee far less aggressively than a forward lunge. Lower until the back knee hovers just above the floor. Front shin stays roughly vertical. Drive back up through the front heel.

Back note: keep your torso upright. A slight forward lean is fine, but don't hinge at the waist. Hands on hips or held at sides.

Single-leg glute bridge glute
3 × 10 each legRest 60 s

Same as the warm-up bridge, but extend one leg straight. This isolates each glute independently and exposes any side-to-side strength difference — common and worth addressing before adding pack weight. The extended leg shouldn't touch the floor. Hips stay level.

Terminal knee extension (isometric) stability
3 × 15 each legRest 45 s

Stand facing the bench, hands resting on it lightly. Slightly bend the working knee (about 20–30°). From that position, squeeze the quad hard to "lock" the knee straight — hold 2 seconds, release. This activates the VMO (inner quad), which is the muscle that most directly controls kneecap tracking and is often undertrained in people with downhill knee pain. Low load, high value.

Seated calf raise calf
3 × 20 each legRest 60 s5 kg dumbbell on thigh

Sit on the bench, dumbbell resting on the lower thigh just above the knee. Raise the heel as high as possible, pause 1 second, lower slowly (3 seconds down). The seated position targets the soleus — the deeper calf muscle that absorbs the bulk of braking force on descents and is underworked by standing calf raises alone.

Cool-down · ~15 min
90/90 hip stretch + standing quad stretch mobility
60 s each position

90/90: sit on the floor with both knees bent at 90°, one in front, one to the side. Sit tall and gently lean over the front shin. Targets the hip external rotators that tighten under pack load. Standing quad: hold the bench, pull one heel toward your glute, keep knees together. 60 seconds each side, twice through.

Add load progressively. Introduce single-leg Romanian deadlifts and lateral step-ups. Begin with 5 kg, move to 8 kg when you can complete all reps with clean form and no back discomfort.

Warm-up · ~15 min
Hip circles + banded lateral walk stability
2 × 15 steps each direction

If you don't have a resistance band, do lateral step-ups instead (step sideways onto the bench). Targets glute medius — the hip abductor that keeps the knee from caving inward on each downhill step. This is directly relevant to your knee issue.

Main block · ~60 min
Weighted step-up with slow step-down eccentric
4 × 10 each legRest 90 s5 → 8 kg dumbbells

Same movement as phase 1, now with dumbbells held at your sides. The 15 kg pack you'll carry is distributed across your whole body; holding 5–8 kg per hand while stepping mimics that load at the legs without compressing the spine. Step-down still 3–4 seconds. This is your most important exercise.

Single-leg Romanian deadlift hamstring/glute
3 × 8 each legRest 90 s5 → 8 kg dumbbell

Hold one dumbbell in the opposite hand to the working leg (e.g. right leg, left hand). Hinge at the hip — push the hip crease backward, keeping a neutral spine, letting the dumbbell track close to the standing leg. The non-working leg extends behind you for balance. Feel the hamstring stretch, then drive the hip forward to stand.

Back safety: this is a hip hinge, not a back bend. If you feel your lower back rounding, reduce the range of motion. Use the bench nearby for a light balance touch if needed. Start with 5 kg and only progress to 8 kg when you're confident in the pattern.

Reverse lunge with dumbbells eccentric
3 × 12 each legRest 90 s5 → 8 kg each hand

Progress from bodyweight with added load. Keep the dumbbells at your sides — never overhead. Torso stays upright. If you feel any lower back twinge, drop back to bodyweight and focus on maintaining a braced core throughout the movement.

Lateral step-up (sideways onto bench) stability
3 × 10 each legRest 60 sBodyweight or 5 kg

Stand beside the bench. Place the near foot fully on the bench sideways, then drive up through that foot to bring both feet to bench level. Step back down with control. This loads the glute medius — critical for knee stability on uneven trail terrain. Keep the knee tracking over the second toe throughout.

Terminal knee extension + seated calf raise stability
3 × 15 / 3 × 20Superset — rest 60 s between pairs

Pair these as a superset: TKEs then straight into seated calf raises. Both are low-load, joint-focused movements — pairing them saves time without fatiguing the main movers. Increase calf raise dumbbell to 8 kg when 5 kg feels easy.

Cool-down · ~15 min
Pigeon pose + calf/Achilles stretch mobility
90 s each side

Pigeon: from all fours, bring one shin forward and lower the back leg straight. This is the deepest hip external rotator stretch available without equipment — the GR20's rocky scrambles will test these muscles constantly. Calf stretch: step one foot back, press the heel flat, hold 60 s for gastrocnemius; then slightly bend the knee and hold 60 s more for the soleus.

Reduce volume, maintain intensity. The goal is to arrive at the trailhead feeling fresh, not fatigued. Two sessions per week maximum. Keep weights the same as peak phase 2 — no need to push harder now.

Condensed session · ~50 min total
Weighted step-up with slow step-down eccentric
3 × 8 each legRest 90 s8 kg dumbbells

Reduce sets by one but keep weight the same. You're maintaining the neuromuscular pattern, not building new capacity. Step-down still slow and controlled.

Single-leg Romanian deadlift hamstring/glute
2 × 8 each legRest 90 s8 kg

Maintain the pattern. If anything feels off in your lower back, drop to 5 kg or skip entirely — protecting your back in the final weeks before the trek is the priority.

Lateral step-up + TKE superset stability
2 × 10 / 2 × 15Rest 60 s

Keep both in as they directly address your knee concern. Low fatigue cost, high maintenance value.

Seated calf raise calf
2 × 20 each legRest 60 s8 kg

Keep this in throughout the taper — calf and Achilles conditioning is one of the last things to show up as a problem on a long trek, so it's worth maintaining right up to departure.

In the final week before the trek: no strength sessions. Walk with a loaded pack on hilly ground if possible. Sleep, eat well, and let the adaptations consolidate.
